I once swore I would never go back. Then I scrolled through an old album, and there it
was, plain as anything: I miss it.

Six years in a city where every direction you pick ends in a park. Wherever you walk
there is ground under your feet instead of concrete, and one lap around the park comes
out at five kilometres. Back home in Vietnam the rhythm is faster. Even *wanting* to slow
down takes effort here.

![The dirt running path along the Neva, lamp posts and old trees on one side, the river on the other](assets/stories/st-petersburg-evenings/01-neva-path.webp)

The path was never anything special. Packed dirt, a line of lamp posts, the water on the
left the whole way. I ran it so many times that I stopped noticing it, which I think is
the thing I actually miss — not the view, but having a place ordinary enough to stop
seeing.

![Late sun coming through bare trees across a tram line, two cars waiting at the kerb](assets/stories/st-petersburg-evenings/02-sunset-through-bare-trees.webp)

> An ice cream, a pastry, a bottle of water before the run. After the run, sitting there
> and watching the light go. Hard to put into words.

That was the whole routine, and it repeated for six years. Nothing about it was worth
writing down at the time, which is exactly why none of it got written down.

![Two people fishing on the embankment as the sun drops behind the Lakhta Center spire](assets/stories/st-petersburg-evenings/03-fishing-at-sunset.webp)

The sun would take its time going down — in summer it would refuse to go down at all —
and there was always someone still fishing. I never learned any of their names. I just
knew they would be there, which was somehow enough.
